A Level Chemistry has a reputation, and it has earned it. Whether your child sits Cambridge (9701), Edexcel (YCH11) or AQA (7405), they face physical, inorganic and organic chemistry examined across multiple papers — including dedicated practical and data-analysis components — and the difference between a B and an A* is usually technique, not intelligence. ## Five things that separate a good chemistry tutor from a name in a directory

**1. They teach your child's exact board — and can prove it.** Cambridge 9701 examines practical planning in its own Paper 5; Edexcel splits content across units with written practical-skills papers; AQA embeds required practicals into the theory exams. A tutor who "teaches A Level chemistry" generically will drill the wrong emphases. Ask which syllabus code they teach and how the papers differ — a real specialist answers instantly.

**2. Past papers are the curriculum, not a garnish.** By the final year, every topic should end with examiner-style questions marked against the actual mark scheme. Mark schemes reward specific phrasing — "partially positive carbon attacked by the nucleophile's lone pair" — and only tutors who work from them constantly teach students to write it.

**3. Live and interactive beats recorded lectures.** Chemistry misconceptions compound silently. A student who watches videos can feel they understand organic mechanisms right up until the mock exam says otherwise. Live classes let a teacher catch the error the week it forms. Recordings should be *included* for revision — not be the product.

**4. Small groups, or genuinely 1-to-1.** "Online class" can mean 40 students on mute. Ask the cap. Small groups (which also cost less than private tutors) keep every student's written work visible to the teacher; that weekly marked homework is where grades actually move.

**5. Progress you can see.** If you won't get reports or marked scripts, you're buying hope, not tuition.
